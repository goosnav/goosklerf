                                    SETTING

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    The fabric of reality has been torn to shreds. A total reality collapse
    event is what the state called it before the state itself disappeared. It
    happened gradually at first, and then all at once.

    A man goes to bed with his wife; he wakes up next to a fish. A truck
    driver barrels down the highway, and a second later the truck and he have
    switched places. Cats bark now. Dogs have forgotten their language.
    Mirrors show rooms that were never there.

    No one knows quite why this happened. Many have their ideas, but ideas
    aren't worth much now that you can't trust the thoughts in your head.

    The chaos left the world a shifting wasteland, but this blank slate may
    be an opportunity in disguise. In the vacuum left after the old order,
    opportunistic warlords have sprung up across the land. There is no return
    to normalcy. No negotiations. No peace in this new world. There is only
    a scramble for whatever resources remain, a scramble to acquire the only
    thing still solid in this world:

                                     Power.

================================================================================
                              HOW TO READ THIS BOOK
================================================================================

    Goosklerf II is a modular card-and-dice game. The same deck can be used
    to play several different game modes, from quick skirmishes to full
    narrative campaigns.

    This rulebook is organized in layers:

        CORE RULES are the rules that are always in effect. Read Parts I
        and II before your first game.

        ADVANCED RULES are optional modules (Part III). Add them one at a
        time as your group gets comfortable. None of them are required.

        GAME MODES (Part V) describe different ways to play using the same
        cards. Each mode tells you which rules to use.

        CAMPAIGN SCENARIOS (Part VI) are ready-to-run adventures for
        Campaign Mode.

    If you are learning the game, read Parts I and II, skim the mode
    selector in Part V, and pick Classic Mode for your first game. Ignore
    everything else until you've played once.



================================================================================
                         PART I - COMPONENTS AND TABLE
================================================================================



1. WHAT YOU NEED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    To play Goosklerf II you need:

        -   A deck of Goosklerf cards (your own, or a shared pool)
        -   At least one six-sided die per player
        -   Additional dice for tracking hit points (recommended: 1 extra
            die per entity in play)
        -   A flat playing surface large enough for the table layout
            (see Section 4)

    Optional but helpful:

        -   A printed play mat for the chosen game mode
        -   Small dice for tracking buffs, rounds, and fortress ownership
        -   A chessboard (only required for Chessklerf mode)



2. CARD TYPES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    There are three card types in Goosklerf II: entities, items, and
    fortresses. Each type has a normal and a rare variant, identified by
    border color.

    ENTITIES (normal: red border / rare: purple border)

        Entities are the fighters. They attack, equip items, occupy
        fortresses, and use special abilities.

        -   Only entities can equip and use items.
        -   Entities may attack enemy entities and enemy fortresses.
        -   An entity is defeated when its hit points reach 0.
        -   Some entities have Special text on the card. Special text
            always overrides the standard rules.

        A Super Entity is a rare entity with two HP dice instead of one.
        Super Entities are marked clearly on the card.

    FORTRESSES (normal: blue border / rare: orange border)

        Fortresses are structures placed in the suburbs. They house
        entities, grant buffs or debuffs to the entities inside, and
        sometimes have special effects.

        -   Fortresses can house up to 3 entities.
        -   Fortresses cannot hold or use items directly.
        -   A fortress is destroyed when its hit points reach 0.
        -   When a fortress is destroyed, all entities inside it are
            destroyed, along with their equipped items.

        A Super Fortress is a rare fortress with three HP dice instead of
        one. Super Fortresses are marked clearly on the card.

    ITEMS (normal: green border / rare: yellow or gold border)

        Items are equipment carried by entities. There are two subtypes:

        Equipped Items stay attached to an entity and provide a continuous
        effect (an attack buff, an HP buff, a special ability, etc.) until
        the item is discarded, transferred, or the entity dies.

        Consumables are used for a one-time effect, then discarded. A
        consumable must be equipped by an entity before it can be used.

        An entity can carry up to 3 items at a time (equipped items and
        consumables combined).

3. CARD ANATOMY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Every card has:

        A NAME at the top.

        A CARD-TYPE ICON indicating whether it is an entity, fortress,
        item, or consumable.

        A BORDER COLOR indicating rarity.

        STAT VALUES. Entities and fortresses have an Attack value and an
        HP value. Items may have buffs or debuffs to those values.

        A TEXT BOX containing any Special rules or flavor text. Special
        rules override general rules if there is a conflict.

    HOW STATS WORK

        ATTACK is the target number you must roll at or below to land a
        normal hit. A base Attack of 3 means you hit on a roll of 1, 2, or
        3 on a six-sided die. Attack can be modified by items, fortress
        buffs, and specials.

        HIT POINTS (HP) is tracked physically with a die placed on top of
        the card. When the card is played, place a die showing its maximum
        HP. Each time it takes damage, rotate the die down by the amount
        of damage taken. When the die reaches 0, the card is defeated.

        Super Entities use two HP dice. Super Fortresses use three. Damage
        is applied one HP at a time across the dice, in any order the
        controlling player chooses.



4. THE TABLE: ZONES AND LAYOUT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    The play area is divided into zones. Know these terms because the
    rules refer to them constantly.

    THE BATTLEFIELD is the center of the table. It is a shared, open space
    where entities fight. All players use one battlefield.

    THE SUBURBS are the fortress zones along the edges of the table. Each
    player has their own suburbs. Fortresses are placed only in the
    suburbs. If you picture the battlefield as the sea, the suburbs are
    the shoreline, and fortresses are ships moored along it.

    GRAVEYARD. Each player has their own graveyard (discard pile).
    Destroyed, defeated, spent, and discarded cards go here unless a rule
    says otherwise.

    DECK. Each player has their own deck. Players keep their deck face
    down nearby. Cards in the deck must be transfered to the HAND before they
    may be placed. 

    HAND. Each player has a private hand of cards. This is a queue of cards
    that can be placed during the Card Play phase.

    SHOP (optional, used with the Shop rule). A face-up row of cards that
    can be purchased during the Card Play phase.

================================================================================
                              PART II - CORE RULES
================================================================================



5. CORE CONCEPTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Before the detailed rules, here are the concepts the rest of the book
    assumes you understand.

    CONTROL. A card is controlled by the player who owns it, unless a
    rule or special effect changes control (for example, capturing a
    fortress).

    OCCUPY. A player occupies a fortress if they have at least one
    surviving entity inside it. A player can control an empty fortress.

    ENGAGEMENT. A single declared combat, from the moment combat is
    declared until one side is destroyed, retreats, or the engagement
    ends by rule. Engagements are resolved one at a time.

    CARD TEXT OVERRIDES. If a card's special text contradicts a general
    rule, the card text wins. If two cards contradict each other, the
    card belonging to the acting player wins.



6. VICTORY CONDITIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Goosklerf II uses three end conditions in multiplayer. The first one
    to trigger ends the game.

    LAST MAN STANDING. Only one player (or team) has any surviving
    entities in play. That player wins immediately.

    LANDLORD. A player becomes Landlord when they control every
    fortress currently in play AND no player has any unplayed fortress
    cards remaining in their deck or hand. Becoming Landlord is not
    an instant win: the player must survive one full round in that
    state to win the game.

    The moment these conditions are met, the player is marked as
    Landlord. Starting from the next player's turn, one full round
    of play begins. If at the end of that round the player is still
    Landlord (still controls every fortress in play, still no
    unplayed fortresses anywhere), they win immediately.

    If during that round any of the following happens, Landlord
    status is broken:
        -   Another player captures one of the Landlord's fortresses.
        -   Another player destroys one of the Landlord's fortresses.
        -   Another player plays a new fortress card into their own
            suburbs.
        -   A scenario effect introduces a new fortress.

    If Landlord status is broken, it can be re-achieved later under
    the same conditions, and the survival round begins again from
    scratch.

    HAMLET. All entities are destroyed, and no player can legally put
    a new entity into play. Everyone loses.

    Scenarios may add additional victory conditions. Scenario victory
    conditions override these defaults.



7. DECK AND STARTING HAND
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    DECK REGIMES. Goosklerf II uses three standardized deck sizes.
    Pick one before the game begins. All players must use the same number of
    cards for their individual decks. 

        SMALL DECK (24 cards)
            Minimum card quantities:
                -   5 entities
                -   1 fortress
		-   10 items

            Shop size: 3 cards (24 cards in deck + 3 in shop)
            Recommended for: quick Classic games, Lightning Tactical
            practice, or playing with small collections.

        MEDIUM DECK (36 cards)
            Minimum card quantities:
                -   10 entities
                -   2 fortresses
                -   15 items
            Shop size: 7 cards (36 cards in deck + 7 in shop)
            Recommended for: default Classic Mode. This is the
            official standard deck size.

        LARGE DECK (54 cards)
            Minimum card quantities:
                -   15 entities
                -   3 fortresses
                -   25 items
            Shop size: 7 cards (54 cards in deck + 7 in shop)
            Recommended for: long Classic Mode sessions, team games,
            Campaign Mode setups, or large battlepaths.

    Decks may contain more entities and fortresses than the minimums
    listed (at the cost of fewer items). Decks may not go below the
    minimums listed.

    You may include at most 3 copies of any card with the same name
    in a single deck.

    STARTING HAND. At the start of the game, each player draws 7
    cards at random from their own deck. This is the only fully
    random draw in the game. Cards drawn on subsequent turns involve
    a choice (see Section 8). If a player's starting deck allows for no
    legal opening card placement, he or she may invoke the "Mulligan Rule".

    MULLIGAN RULE. If a player's starting hand allows for no opening card
    placement (i.e. no entities or fortresses in starting hand), then he
    or she may invoke the "Mulligan Rule" where all players must reshuffle
    their decks and draw new random decks. 

    FIRST PLAYER. Randomly determine the first player by die roll or
    agreement. Play proceeds clockwise. A round ends when every
    surviving player has taken one turn.



8. TURN STRUCTURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    A turn consists of five phases, taken in order.

        1. DRAW
        2. CARD PLAY
        3. MOVEMENT
        4. COMBAT
        5. VICTORY CHECK

    Each phase must be completed before moving to the next.

    PHASE 1: DRAW

        Draw the top 2 cards of your deck at random. Then look through the
	entire deck and select 1 deliberately. Add all 3 cards to your hand. 
	Thoroughly reshuffle your deck after drawing up to 3 cards. 

        The kept card is immediately playable this turn.

        Exception: On the very first turn of the game, players skip
        the Draw phase. They already drew 7 cards as their starting
        hand.

        If fewer than 2 cards remain in your deck, draw 1 (keep it)
        or 0 (skip the phase). If exactly 2 cards remain, draw both
        and keep 1 as normal.

    PHASE 2: CARD PLAY

        The active player must play exactly 3 cards from their hand.

        A card is played when it is:
            -   a fortress placed into your own suburbs
            -   an entity placed onto the battlefield
            -   an entity placed into one of your own fortresses
            -   an item equipped onto one of your entities
            -   a shop purchase, if the Shop rule is active

        If you cannot play 3 legal cards from your hand, you must
        discard cards from your hand until cards played plus cards
        discarded equals 3. (Example: you can only play 2 cards, so
        you discard 1.)

        This forced-play rule is waived only if your hand is empty
        at the start of Card Play. If your hand is empty, skip to
        Movement.

        Capacity limits still apply: 3 items per entity, 3 entities
        per fortress, 4 entities on the battlefield per player.

    PHASE 3: MOVEMENT

        You may freely reposition your own entities and transfer your
        own items during this phase, as long as all resulting positions
        are legal.

        Legal movement:
            -   Battlefield to one of your fortresses (with space)
            -   Your fortress to the battlefield (if legal battlefield
                room exists)
            -   Your fortress to another of your fortresses (with space)

        Items may be freely transferred between your own entities during
        this phase, as long as capacity limits are respected.

        Fortresses cannot hold items. If you need to move an item
        somewhere, it must ride on an entity.

    PHASE 4: COMBAT

        You may declare any number of combat engagements this turn. Each
        engagement is resolved fully before the next begins. Each of your
        entities may participate in only one engagement per turn, unless
        a special ability says otherwise.

        See Section 9 for combat rules.

    PHASE 5: VICTORY CHECK

        After all combat is resolved, check whether any victory
        condition has been met (Section 6). If so, the game ends.
        Landlord status is checked and tracked here (see Section 6
        for the survival-round rule).



9. COMBAT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Combat begins when the active player declares an engagement. The
    player declaring combat is the attacker. All opposing entities
    involved are the defenders.

    WHO CAN ATTACK WHOM

        Battlefield entities may attack:
            -   Enemy battlefield entities
            -   Enemy fortresses in the suburbs (fortress assault)

        Fortress entities may attack:
            -   Enemy entities on the battlefield, but only once per
                engagement, as an Initial Volley (see below)
            -   Enemies attacking their fortress, during that assault
            -   Enemy fortresses, only if the Fortress Barrage rule is
                active (see Section 20)

    INITIAL VOLLEY (FORTRESS ENTITIES)

        When a battlefield engagement is declared, any non-engaged
        fortress whose occupants wish to intervene may fire a single
        volley into the engagement.

        Key limits:

            -   ONE VOLLEY PER FORTRESS. A fortress may contribute at
                most one volley shot per battlefield engagement,
                regardless of how many entities are inside it. The
                player controlling the fortress picks which occupant
                fires.

            -   FORTRESSES UNDER ASSAULT CANNOT VOLLEY. If a fortress
                is itself currently being assaulted (its occupants are
                in the engagement as defenders), it cannot also volley
                into that or any other engagement. Its occupants are
                already fighting.

            -   ONCE PER TURN PER FORTRESS. A fortress that has
                already volleyed during the current player's turn
                cannot volley again that turn, even into a different
                engagement.

        The volley shot is a single normal attack against any one
        attacker of the volleying player's choice, resolved before the
        first combat round begins. The volleying fortress is not
        considered part of the engagement: its occupants cannot be
        targeted by the attackers in return, and they do not count
        toward the one-engagement-per-turn limit for that entity.

        Volleys are fortress covering fire. They do not trigger Victory
        Rewards. They cannot be parried.

    COMBAT ROUNDS

        Combat proceeds in alternating rounds. In each round:

            1. The attacker's participating entities each take one action.
            2. The defender's participating entities each take one action.
            3. If one side has been fully destroyed, the engagement ends.
               Otherwise, begin the next round.

        Actions available to each entity on its turn:

            A. Normal attack (Section 9.1)
            B. Use a consumable
            C. Use a special ability from its card
            D. Parry, if the Parry rule is active (Section 13)
            E. Retreat, if the Retreat rule is active (Section 14)
            F. Item swap, if that rule is active (Section 16)

    9.1 NORMAL ATTACK

        1. Declare a target. The attacker chooses one legal enemy target
           in this engagement (one entity, or the fortress if this is a
           fortress assault).

        2. Roll 1d6.

        3. If the result is equal to or below the acting entity's current
           modified Attack value, the attack hits. Otherwise it misses.

        4. A normal hit deals 1 HP of damage unless a special rule or
           card text changes the amount.

    9.2 FORTRESS ASSAULT

        In a fortress assault, attacking battlefield entities may target
        either the fortress or any defending entity inside it. Defending
        entities inside the fortress may target attackers normally.

        A fortress assault ends when:
            -   All defending entities are destroyed, OR
            -   All attackers are destroyed or retreat, OR
            -   The fortress is destroyed.

        CAPTURING A FORTRESS. If all defending entities are destroyed and
        the fortress still has at least 1 HP, the surviving attackers may
        choose to capture or destroy the fortress.

            CAPTURE: Control of the fortress transfers to the attacker.
            Up to the fortress's capacity in attackers may move inside
            it; any excess attackers stay on the battlefield. Attackers
            who just captured a fortress must remain inside it until
            their next Movement phase.

            DESTROY: Discard the fortress. No one controls it. Attackers
            remain on the battlefield.

        DESTROYING A FORTRESS. If the fortress itself is reduced to 0 HP
        during the assault, it is destroyed. All entities inside it, and
        their equipped items, are immediately destroyed with it. (If the
        Last Stand rule is active, defenders may take their final action
        before discarding.)



10. DAMAGE, DEFEAT, AND VICTORY REWARDS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    10.1 APPLYING DAMAGE

        When an entity or fortress takes damage, rotate its HP die down
        by the amount taken. Healing works in reverse but can never raise
        an HP die above its printed maximum unless a card explicitly
        says so.

    10.2 HP DOES NOT RESET

        Damage persists between combats. An entity that survives one
        engagement with 2 HP remaining enters its next engagement still
        at 2 HP. The only way to heal is through an item, consumable, or
        special ability that explicitly restores HP. This makes combat
        matter and keeps games decisive.

    10.3 DEFEAT

        An entity is defeated when its HP reaches 0. Place the entity
        and all of its equipped items in its owner's graveyard.

        A fortress is destroyed when its HP reaches 0. Place the fortress
        in its owner's graveyard, along with every entity inside it and
        every item those entities were carrying.

    10.4 ITEM OWNERSHIP

        When a card is discarded, it returns to the graveyard of its
        original owner, not the graveyard of whoever was holding it.
        This prevents item-theft ambiguity. The only exception is the
        Scavenger rule (Section 18), which is optional.

    10.5 VICTORY REWARDS (FIRST STRIKE INCENTIVE)

        Aggression is rewarded. When an engagement ends:

            IF THE ATTACKER WINS (destroys all defenders, captures or
            destroys the fortress, or otherwise achieves the engagement
            objective):
                -   The attacker draws 1 card at random from the top
                    of their deck and adds it to their hand. The card
                    is not playable this turn; it becomes available on
                    the attacker's next turn like any other hand card.

            IF THE DEFENDER WINS (destroys all attackers, or all
            attackers retreat without destroying all defenders):
                -   The defender receives no reward.

            If the attacker's deck is empty, no card is drawn.

        Victory Rewards are not triggered by fortress volleys, by
        Fortress Barrages (Section 20), or by draws in which both
        sides are wiped out. The reward exists specifically to make
        declaring battlefield combat a better choice than sitting
        still.



11. STAT LIMITS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Unless a card explicitly overrides them:

            STAT              MINIMUM     MAXIMUM
            Attack               1           5
            HP (per die)         0           6
            Items per entity     0           3
            Entities per fort    0           3
            Battlefield entities 0           4 per player

    If a buff would push a value above its cap, it stops at the cap. If
    a debuff would push a value below its minimum, it stops at the
    minimum (except HP, which can reach 0 and defeat the card).

    If a change in capacity would leave an entity or fortress holding
    more than the new maximum, the controlling player immediately chooses
    which cards remain legal and discards the excess to their graveyard.



================================================================================
                     PART III - ADVANCED RULES (OPTIONAL)
================================================================================


    These are modular rules. Add any of them to your game if all players
    agree before the game begins. Each one is self-contained. None are
    required.



12. SHOP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    At the start of the game, each player draws cards at random from
    their deck and places them face-up in their own shop area. The
    number of shop cards depends on the deck regime (see Section 7):

            Small Deck:    3 shop cards
            Medium Deck:   7 shop cards
            Large Deck:    7 shop cards

    Shop cards belong to the player who placed them. Only that player
    may buy from their own shop. All shop cards are always visible to
    all players throughout the game.

    BUYING. During your Card Play phase, you may purchase one or more
    shop cards. To buy 1 shop card, do one of the following:

        -   Discard 2 normal cards from your hand, OR
        -   Discard 1 rare card from your hand (rare = purple entity,
            orange fortress, or yellow/gold item).

    A purchased card must be played or equipped immediately using the
    normal placement rules. If it cannot be legally placed, it cannot
    be purchased. Each shop purchase counts as one of your 3 played
    cards for the turn.

    The shop does not refill on its own. Once a shop card is bought,
    that slot is gone for the rest of the game unless a special rule
    refills it.



13. PARRY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Instead of taking a normal action during its turn in combat, an
    entity may declare a Parry.

    Choose one normal hit that was landed on this entity during the
    previous enemy round. Roll 1d6. If the roll is equal to or below
    the parrying entity's Attack stat, the parry succeeds: the entity
    regains 1 HP lost from that hit.

    Parry only cancels 1 HP of damage from a single normal attack. It
    cannot cancel damage from specials, consumables, or extra damage
    bonuses from other rules.

    A parry attempt consumes the entity's action for that round,
    whether it succeeds or fails. An entity can only parry one hit at
    a time.



14. RETREAT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Instead of attacking on its turn in combat, an entity may declare
    a retreat. The entity announces its intent to retreat and takes no
    action this round.

    During the next round, enemy entities in the engagement may target
    the retreating entity normally. If the retreating entity survives
    that round, it leaves the engagement at the end of the round.

    A retreating entity must have a legal destination:
        -   Battlefield entities may retreat into a friendly fortress
            that has room.
        -   Fortress entities under assault may retreat into the
            battlefield if battlefield room exists, or into another
            friendly fortress if one has room.

    If no legal destination exists, the entity cannot retreat.



15. LAST STAND
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Defenders only. When a defending entity is reduced to 0 HP, it may
    take one final action before being discarded: a normal attack, a
    consumable, or a special ability.

    After taking its final action, the entity is placed in its owner's
    graveyard along with its items. Attackers do not get Last Stand.
    An entity killed by Last Stand is defeated normally.



16. ITEM SWAP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Instead of taking any other action in combat, an entity may transfer
    up to two of its equipped items to another friendly entity in the
    same engagement. The acting entity does not attack this round. The
    receiving entity must have room for the incoming items.

    Items cannot be transferred to enemies. Consumables that have
    already been activated cannot be transferred.



17. REINFORCEMENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    At the end of either side's combat round, that side may call one
    reinforcement into the engagement.

    The reinforcing entity must come from a legal nearby zone:
        -   One of your own fortresses in the suburbs
        -   The battlefield (for engagements involving your battlefield
            entities or a fortress assault on your own fortress)

    A reinforcement enters the engagement immediately and can be
    targeted by enemies in the next round. It cannot act during the
    round it arrives. After the engagement ends, the reinforcement
    remains wherever it reinforced into: battlefield reinforcements
    stay on the battlefield, fortress reinforcements stay in that
    fortress.

    You cannot reinforce into a location that is already at its cap:
    no reinforcement into a fortress that already holds 3 entities, no
    reinforcement onto a battlefield already holding 4 of your entities.

    Only one reinforcement per side per round.



18. SCAVENGER
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    If a friendly entity is defeated during an engagement, allied
    entities in that same engagement may immediately equip the dead
    entity's items if they have capacity. Any items that cannot be
    equipped are discarded normally.

    Items that were already equipped by other entities cannot be
    transferred to a dead entity to "save" them for scavenging. This
    rule only applies at the moment of defeat.



19. SUPERCHARGED ATTACK
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Instead of a normal attack, an entity may declare a Supercharged
    Attack. Roll 3d6 instead of 1d6.

        -   If all three dice are equal to or below the entity's Attack
            value, the attack deals 2 damage instead of 1.
        -   If any one die misses, the attack fails and the attacking
            entity takes 1 self-damage.

    A Supercharged Attack consumes the entity's action for the round.



20. FORTRESS BARRAGE (FORTRESS-VS-FORTRESS COMBAT)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Think of fortresses like ships fighting across open water. A
    fortress with occupants may engage an enemy fortress directly,
    without moving through the battlefield.

    To declare a Fortress Barrage:
        -   The attacking fortress must have at least one entity inside.
        -   The target is a single enemy fortress.
        -   Entities inside the attacking fortress may target only the
            target fortress, not its occupants.
        -   Defending entities inside the target fortress may target
            attacking entities inside the attacking fortress normally
            (they fire back).
        -   Combat resolves in alternating rounds like a standard
            battlefield engagement.

    A fortress cannot be captured through a Fortress Barrage. Only
    battlefield assaults can capture a fortress. If the Barrage kills
    all of the target fortress's occupants without destroying the
    fortress itself, the fortress stands empty under its previous
    controller's ownership.

    If the target fortress is destroyed, all its occupants and their
    items are destroyed with it.



================================================================================
                PART IV - BACKWARDS COMPATIBILITY (GOOSKLERF I)
================================================================================


    Goosklerf II simplified the stat system from Goosklerf I (GKI).
    GKI used Attack, Defense, and Capacity stats. GKII uses only Attack
    and HP, with a fixed capacity of 3. When you play with a mixed
    deck, follow these conversions.

    DEFENSE CONVERSION. When a GKI card references Defense in GKII
    play, treat the Defense value as Attack when that entity is
    defending (not the initiator of combat). If a GKI fortress buffs
    Defense on a GKII entity, apply the buff to that entity's Attack
    stat instead.

    CAPACITY CONVERSION. GKI cards retain their printed capacity
    values. GKII cards use a fixed capacity of 3 items per entity and
    3 entities per fortress, regardless of any GKI capacity effects
    that might target them.

        -   If a GKI effect would raise a GKII entity's capacity, the
            GKII entity's capacity stays at 3.
        -   If a GKI effect would raise another GKI card's capacity,
            apply it normally using GKI rules.
        -   Overflow from mid-battle capacity changes is handled as in
            Section 11: the controlling player picks which cards to
            discard.

    MIXED ITEM BUFFS. If a GKI item buffs both Attack and Defense, use
    the relevant value based on whether the holding entity is attacking
    or defending during that combat round.

    LEGACY OFF. You may also simply ignore Defense and Capacity on GKI
    cards and play them as pure GKII cards. This is the cleanest option
    for casual play.



================================================================================
                             PART V - GAME MODES
================================================================================



21. MODE SELECTOR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Goosklerf II has five official modes.

    CLASSIC MODE. The flagship multiplayer experience. Full rules, full
    decks, full war. 2 to 4 players.

    TACTICAL MODE. A smaller, faster, more structured battle with no
    deck refill and fixed starting loadouts. 2 to 4 players. Three deck-
    size variants: Lightning (tiny), Standard Tactical (medium), and
    Skirmish (overpowered).

    SINGLEPLAYER MODE. A solo battlepath game where you fight through
    a series of enemy fortresses. 1 player.

    CAMPAIGN MODE. A narrative, RPG-like mode with a Dungeon Master and
    scripted scenarios. 1 DM plus 1 to 4 players.

    CHESSKLERF. A positional variant played on a chessboard. 2 players.

    Each mode tells you which core and advanced rules to use.



22. CLASSIC MODE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Classic Mode is the standard way to play Goosklerf II. 2 to 4
    players. Last player standing, or first to Landlord, wins.

    SETUP

        All players agree on a deck regime before setup (see
        Section 7):

            -   SMALL (24 cards) for a fast game.
            -   MEDIUM (36 cards) is the default Classic experience.
            -   LARGE (54 cards) for long grinding sessions.

        Each player builds a legal deck of the chosen regime size,
        respecting the minimum composition rules in Section 7.

        Each player draws 7 random cards as their opening hand.

        If the Shop rule is in effect, each player sets up their
        Shop now (3 cards for Small, 7 cards for Medium or Large).

        No player begins with any cards on the table. Fortresses must
        be played from hand into the suburbs during Card Play.

        Randomly determine the first player. Play proceeds clockwise.

    RULES TO USE

        -   All Core Rules (Part II)
        -   Shop (recommended)
        -   Parry, Retreat, Last Stand, Item Swap (recommended)
        -   Reinforcements, Fortress Barrage (recommended)
        -   Scavenger, Supercharged Attack (optional)

        First-time players may skip the advanced modules entirely and
        play with only the Core Rules plus Shop. Add complexity later.

    VICTORY

        Standard Victory Conditions (Section 6): Last Man Standing,
        Landlord (requires surviving one full round after claiming
        Landlord status), or Hamlet (loss for all).



23. TACTICAL MODE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Tactical Mode is a fixed-loadout battle with no deck refill. Every
    card you get is what you start with. 2 to 4 players.

    Tactical Mode comes in three deck-size variants. Choose one before
    starting.

    23.1 LIGHTNING (FAST)

        Each player gets:
            -   3 entities
            -   1 fortress
            -   3 items (distributed as desired, up to 1 per entity to
                start)

        No deck, no hand, no refill. At setup, place your fortress in
        your suburbs and your entities either on the battlefield or in
        your fortress.

        Each round, players take turns performing Movement and Combat
        phases only. No Card Play, no Refill.

        Recommended rules: Core only. Retreat and Parry optional.

    23.2 STANDARD TACTICAL

        Each player gets:
            -   4 entities
            -   1 fortress
            -   8 items selected before the game begins
            -   7-item reserve hand (the items not yet equipped)

        At setup, place your fortress in your suburbs and your entities
        on the battlefield or in your fortress. Each entity may begin
        with up to 2 equipped items.

        Turn structure is Movement, Combat, then a special Tactical
        Phase where you may equip any items from your reserve hand to
        your entities (respecting capacity). No deck, no refill.

        Recommended rules: Core plus Retreat, Parry, Item Swap, and
        Reinforcements.

    23.3 SKIRMISH (OVERPOWERED)

        Each player gets:
            -   3 entities
            -   1 fortress
            -   12 items (any combination)

        During this mode only, the item capacity cap is raised to 4 per
        entity. Each player loads their entities to the teeth at the
        start. No deck, no hand, no refill.

        This mode is a single, brutal battle. Whoever has surviving
        entities at the end wins. No Shop. No refill phase.

        Recommended rules: All advanced rules enabled.

    VICTORY (ALL VARIANTS)

        Eliminate all enemy entities OR destroy the enemy fortress.
        Tactical Mode does not use Landlord or Hamlet.


    [ILLUSTRATION: Three small tactical layouts side by side, one for
    each variant. Each shows player slots, fortress, entity zone, and
    item reserve (where applicable). Small and clean, not detailed.]



24. SINGLEPLAYER MODE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Singleplayer Mode is a solo battlepath game. You fight through a
    chain of enemy-held fortresses, keeping whatever items and entities
    you can salvage along the way.

    24.1 SETUP

        Sort your deck into three piles: fortresses, entities, items.
        Shuffle each pile separately and place all three face-down.

        Build the battlepath face-down by drawing fortresses and
        entities blindly and placing them at each level. The level
        compositions are:

            Level 1:  1 fortress, 1 entity, no items
            Level 2:  1 fortress, 1 entity with 1 item
            Level 3:  1 fortress, 2 entities with 1 item each
            Level 4:  1 fortress, 3 entities with 1 item each
            Level 5:  1 fortress, 3 entities with 2 items each
            Level 6:  1 fortress, 3 entities with 3 items each
            Level 7+: 2 fortresses with 3 entities per fortress, each
                      with 3 items (repeat as long as you have cards)

        Do not look at the face-down enemy cards as you place them.

        After building the battlepath, choose 3 entities from the
        remaining cards for your starting strike force. Draw 3 item
        cards at random and equip them to your strike force as you
        wish.

        Shuffle the remaining items and entities together to form your
        reserve deck. Reveal 7 cards from the top of that deck into
        your shop. The shop is always face-up and always visible.

    24.2 TURN FLOW

        Attack the next level on the battlepath. Flip the fortress
        and all entities inside face-up, and resolve the combat as
        a fortress assault.

        If you defeat the fortress and all its defenders, go to the
        Post-Battle phase below.

        If any of your entities die, remove them. If all of your
        entities die, the game ends in defeat.

    24.3 ENEMY AI

        Enemy entities behave like this, in order:

            1. If an enemy has an equipped consumable: think of a
               number from 1 to 6 in your head (do not speak it).
               Roll 1d6. If the die matches your number, the enemy
               uses the consumable on a legal target, and it is
               revealed.

            2. Otherwise the enemy performs a normal attack.

        ENEMY TARGETING. Enemies target your entity with the lowest
        current HP. In case of a tie, they target the entity with the
        highest Attack. In case of a further tie, choose randomly.

        ENEMY ITEMS ARE HIDDEN. Keep enemy equipped items face-down
        until the psychic roll above triggers their use, at which point
        flip them face-up and resolve normally. This keeps enemy loadouts
        mysterious until you fight.

        BOSS FORTRESSES. Every third fortress on the battlepath
        (Level 3, 6, 9, etc.) is a Boss Fortress. Its final surviving
        entity gains Last Stand automatically, even if you are not
        otherwise playing with Last Stand.

        ENEMY REINFORCEMENTS. At the start of each enemy combat round
        at Level 4 or higher, roll 1d6. On a 6, one entity from the
        NEXT level on the battlepath is pulled forward and deployed
        into the current fortress as reinforcement (if the fortress
        has room). Flip this entity face-up and equip it with whatever
        items it was carrying. Remove it from its original level. If
        the next level is empty, no reinforcement is deployed.

    24.4 POST-BATTLE

        After you defeat a fortress:

            1. Add the fortress to your trophy pile.
            2. You may take any items carried by the defeated enemies,
               up to your strike force's item capacity. Discard the
               rest.
            3. You may make shop purchases. Spending costs:
                    -   2 normal cards = 1 shop card
                    -   1 rare card = 1 shop card
               Fortresses in your trophy pile may be spent as currency
               at the same rates. Spent trophy fortresses are removed
               from your trophy pile (and from your final score).
            4. The shop does not refill on its own.

        Purchased entities join your strike force. Purchased items are
        equipped immediately (or discarded if you have no room).

        If you purchased nothing, you cannot refill entities mid-run
        except by scavenging enemy items.

    24.5 SCORING

        Final score is calculated after the game ends, whether by
        victory or defeat.

            Each level cleared:                         100 points
            Each trophy fortress remaining unspent:      50 points
            Each surviving entity:                       25 points
            Each surviving equipped item:                10 points

        Optional rarity modifier for fortresses in the trophy pile:
            Super fortress:   x3
            Rare fortress:    x2
            Normal fortress:  x1



25. CAMPAIGN MODE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Campaign Mode is the RPG mode of Goosklerf II. One player is the
    Dungeon Master. The other players (1 to 4) are Adventurers. The
    DM runs a scenario; the players play through it using their cards
    as character sheets.

    This is a rules-light system. Most of the flavor comes from the
    scenario, not from mechanical complexity.

    25.1 CHARACTERS

        Each player chooses 1 entity as their character. This entity
        card is their character sheet. The player's starting stats
        (Attack, HP) are the stats printed on the card.

        Each player is also dealt 3 items and 1 consumable. These are
        the character's starting gear. Equip as many as capacity (3)
        allows; hold the rest in a personal reserve hand.

        Character progression comes from leveling (Section 25.5).

    25.2 THE DUNGEON MASTER

        The DM represents the world, the enemies, and the story. The
        DM's job is to:

            -   Describe each scene and let players react.
            -   Run all enemies in combat, rolling attacks and making
                target decisions.
            -   Adjudicate non-combat actions (see Section 25.3).
            -   Advance the story between encounters.
            -   Decide when characters level up.

        The DM uses cards from the shared deck as enemies, fortresses,
        rooms, loot, and set dressing. Pull a fortress card to represent
        a significant location. Pull entity cards to represent any
        hostile encounter. Pull item cards as loot.

    25.3 NON-COMBAT ACTIONS (CHECKS)

        When a character attempts something uncertain outside combat
        (picking a lock, persuading a stranger, sprinting across broken
        terrain, remembering something), the DM sets a difficulty:

            Easy       roll 5 or less on 1d6
            Normal     roll 4 or less on 1d6
            Hard       roll 3 or less on 1d6
            Desperate  roll 2 or less on 1d6

        The player rolls 1d6. On success, the action works. On failure,
        the DM describes the consequence.

        Items and specials may modify the difficulty by +1 or -1 at
        the DM's discretion.

    25.4 COMBAT IN CAMPAIGN MODE

        Combat uses standard Goosklerf II attack rules (roll under
        Attack to hit). The DM controls all enemy turns.

        VARIABLE DAMAGE. Unlike standard Goosklerf II, a successful
        combat hit in Campaign Mode deals variable damage:

            1-3 on 1d6:   1 damage (a scrape)
            4-5 on 1d6:   2 damage (a solid hit)
            6   on 1d6:   3 damage (a devastating blow)

        Roll this damage die after every successful hit, for both
        players and enemies. This makes fights swingier and more
        cinematic.

        The DM may override damage for dramatic effect: a boss might
        always deal a minimum of 2; a glancing shot might deal 0.

        Player HP persists between encounters, as in Classic Mode.
        Healing comes from items, consumables, specials, or rest
        between acts (DM's call).

    25.5 LEVELING

        Characters level up at the end of each major act of the
        scenario, or whenever the DM decides the players have earned
        it.

            Level 2:  +1 maximum HP
            Level 3:  Once per session, reroll one failed roll
            Level 4:  +1 Attack (up to the maximum of 5)
            Level 5:  Squire Slot: you may assign one extra entity
                      from your reserve as a squire. The squire holds
                      1 item and absorbs damage in your place: when
                      you would take damage, you may assign it to the
                      squire first. If the squire dies, you lose it.
            Level 6:  +1 maximum HP
            Level 7:  +1 item capacity (4 instead of 3)
            Level 8+: Alternate between +1 HP, +1 reroll per session,
                      and one DM-approved custom perk.

    25.6 STRUCTURE OF A SCENARIO

        Every official Campaign scenario is structured in 4 acts:

            Act I:    Entrance. Set the scene, establish the goal,
                      introduce the weird local rule.
            Act II:   Complication. A new threat or moral decision
                      raises the stakes.
            Act III:  Collapse. Pressure builds. Resources tighten.
                      Enemies escalate.
            Act IV:   Finale. Confront the core problem. Win, lose, or
                      make a costly choice.

        Between acts, characters may level up and (at the DM's
        discretion) rest to restore HP.

    25.7 DEATH AND REVIVAL

        A character reduced to 0 HP is not dead unless the scenario
        says so. They are downed. At the end of the encounter, if
        their allies survive, the character recovers with 1 HP.

        If the character is downed during Act IV, or during a
        scripted "no second chances" beat, they die permanently.

        This is softer than standard Goosklerf II because Campaign
        Mode is about story, not attrition.



26. CHESSKLERF
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Chessklerf is a positional variant for 2 players. It combines
    Goosklerf combat with chess movement. You need a chessboard, 4
    chess pieces per player (queen, knight, rook, bishop) and the
    pawns from a standard chess set, plus Goosklerf cards.

    Chessklerf is not backwards-compatible with standard chess rules.
    Ignore everything you know about castling, en passant, and check.

    26.1 SETUP

        Each player selects or draws 4 entities to be their "major
        pieces" and places one chess piece on top of each entity card
        to designate its role:

            Queen  (fastest, most dangerous)
            Knight (jumps)
            Rook   (orthogonal long-range)
            Bishop (diagonal long-range)

        If you do not have a queen piece available, use a king as a
        stand-in.

        Each major piece may be equipped with up to 2 items from your
        hand before the game begins.

        Place the 4 major pieces on your back rank. The two corner
        squares of your back rank are fortresses: any major piece
        sitting on a corner square is considered to occupy that
        fortress. The fortresses grant standard fortress buffs (use
        a simple shared fortress card for both corner fortresses, or
        draw one per player at setup).

        Place your pawns in the middle of the board. Each player's
        pawns occupy their side of the middle two ranks (ranks 4 and
        5 of the standard 8x8 board, not ranks 2 and 7). This is the
        "opposite of regular chess" setup: pawns start facing each
        other directly across the center, major pieces at the back.

        Decide who moves first by coin flip or agreement.

    26.2 TURNS

        On your turn, you may:
            -   Move one major piece according to its chess movement
                rules, OR
            -   Move one pawn forward one square, OR
            -   Do nothing (pass).

        Major pieces move exactly as they do in chess, with no limit
        on distance for queens, rooks, and bishops (blocked by other
        pieces normally), and knights jumping L-shapes.

        A piece on a corner fortress square may begin its turn by
        moving up to 2 squares out of the fortress regardless of its
        normal rules, representing the fortress launching the piece.

    26.3 CAPTURING AND COMBAT

        PAWN CAPTURE. A pawn captures enemy pieces as in chess
        (diagonally forward one square). A pawn capture is resolved
        instantly with no combat: the captured piece is defeated and
        removed. The pawn stays on its new square. As a reward for
        the capture, draw 1 item card from a side item pile (set
        aside at the start of the game). If the pawn can somehow
        equip it, the pawn keeps the item. Otherwise discard it.

        MAJOR PIECE CAPTURE. When a major piece moves onto a square
        occupied by an enemy piece, combat is initiated between the
        two pieces (not resolved instantly). Use standard Goosklerf
        II combat rules, attacker-first. The attacker is the piece
        that moved.

            IF THE ATTACKER WINS, the defending piece is removed and
            the attacker ends its turn on the captured square.

            IF THE DEFENDER WINS, the attacker is removed and the
            defender stays on its square.

            IF THE ATTACKER RETREATS successfully (using the standard
            Retreat rule), the attacker returns to any legal square
            it could have moved to instead of the capture square.

        FORTRESS CAPTURE. If a major piece enters an enemy corner
        fortress square, combat is initiated as above. If the
        attacker wins, they capture the fortress.

    26.4 PAWN PROMOTION (EXPLOSION)

        If a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board (the enemy's
        back rank), it explodes. Remove the pawn and every piece,
        pawn, or fortress in a 3x3 square centered on the exploding
        pawn's square. This includes friendly pieces. Exploded
        fortresses are destroyed.

        This rule makes pawn advancement enormously dangerous for
        both sides, and is the game's primary tactical wildcard.

    26.5 VICTORY

        You win Chessklerf if:
            -   You capture both of the enemy's corner fortresses, OR
            -   All enemy major pieces are destroyed.

        If both players lose all their major pieces in the same turn
        (for example, a mutual pawn explosion), the game is a draw.
        Nothing matters anymore anyway.

    corner fortresses on each player's back rank (a1, h1, a8, h8),
    four major pieces on the back rank between them (b1, c1, f1,
    g1 and symmetric for black), pawns on ranks 4 and 5 facing each

                         PART VI - CAMPAIGN SCENARIOS

    The following scenarios are for Campaign Mode (Section 25). Each
    one runs in 4 acts and can be played in a single session or
    broken into multiple sessions. Read the whole scenario before
    running it.



SCENARIO A: THE RADIO STATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    FLAVOR

    Somewhere in the district, a transmitter is still broadcasting.
    Nobody is sure what it's saying anymore, and nobody is sure who
    is transmitting. What is sure is that the people who hear it
    start behaving wrong. Their eyes go flat. They stop answering
    questions. They begin to gather.

    The party found out about it through a courier. A woman on a
    bicycle, wheels wrapped in duct tape, riding hard from the
    direction of the tower. She skidded to a stop in front of them,
    handed over a tape recorder still warm from her pocket, and
    said "make it stop" with a mouth full of blood. Then she got
    back on the bicycle and rode off the same way she had come,
    which was toward the tower, which was where the blood was
    coming from.

    The tape is blank. The recorder is still running.

    SETUP

    The players begin this scenario outside the broadcast district.
    Each character has full HP and their starting loadout. One
    character carries the tape recorder. It cannot be discarded.

    Draw one rare fortress from the deck. This is the Radio Station.
    Set it aside. Draw 5 entities from the deck as the enemy pool
    the DM will deploy over the course of the scenario.

    Throughout the scenario, the tape recorder occasionally plays
    back audio that has not been recorded yet. Use this freely for
    flavor: a scream the party has not screamed, a door closing in
    a room the party has not entered, one of the characters saying
    something they are going to say later in the session.

    ACT I - ENTRANCE

    Objective: Cross the district checkpoint.

    The party approaches a narrow road blocked by stacked cars. Two
    checkpoint guards (pull 2 enemies from the pool) wave them down.
    The guards have the flat eyes. They are polite. They keep asking
    if the party has a permit. They keep asking the same way, with
    the same intonation, as if their voices are being routed through
    something that is not their brain.

    The party may:
        -   Fight them (standard combat, party acts first).
        -   Talk their way through (Normal check, 4 or less on 1d6).
            On success, the guards step aside and stare at a spot
            ten feet behind the party until the party is gone.
            Failure triggers combat with the guards already hostile.
        -   Distract one guard by playing the tape recorder at them.
            The guard becomes catatonic for the rest of the scene.
            The other guard must still be dealt with.

    LOCAL RULE - THE BROADCAST IS EVERYWHERE. Any character who
    spends a whole round in this scenario without attacking, moving,
    or speaking must roll 1d6. On a 1, they begin to hear the
    broadcast clearly. Their Attack is reduced by 1 until the end
    of the next round. If a character fails this roll three times
    in one scenario, they become a listener: they stop taking
    actions and must be carried out by their allies. A carried
    listener cannot fight but takes up a party slot. They recover
    after the scenario if the broadcast is ended.

    ACT II - COMPLICATION

    Objective: Find three power cells to reach the top of the tower.

    The broadcast tower's ground-floor elevator is dead. To climb,
    the party needs three working power cells. There are three
    sources in the district.

    SOURCE 1: A SQUAT CONCRETE BUILDING. A bureaucratic holdout:
    people who still believe they work for a government that no
    longer exists. Draw 1 normal fortress as the building. Place
    3 enemies inside. The bureaucrats have one power cell in a
    locked drawer. They will not give it up without either:
        -   A fortress assault (standard rules).
        -   Paperwork. They will hand over the cell if presented
            with any card with writing on it, treated as a "permit"
            with ceremonial solemnity. The party sacrifices 1 item
            card of their choice, handed to the head bureaucrat.
        -   A Hard check (3 or less) to outtalk them.

    SOURCE 2: A CRASHED AMBULANCE. The ambulance still has its
    engine running. It has been running for weeks. One power cell
    is under the hood, but the ambulance's driver is still in the
    front seat, mostly, and still trying to drive. A Hard check
    to reach the engine compartment without the driver noticing.
    On failure, combat with 1 enemy (the driver, pulled from the
    pool, with +1 HP).

    SOURCE 3: A CHILD. A child on a swingset in an empty park is
    holding the third cell like a toy. The child is calm. The child
    is not a listener but is also not a normal child. The child
    will trade the cell for any shiny item (sacrifice 1 item card).
    Refusing to trade and taking the cell by force is allowed but
    has consequences: at the start of Act IV, the child appears at
    the top of the tower as a hostile entity with Attack 4, HP 4,
    and a special that ignores parries.

    ACT III - COLLAPSE

    Objective: Climb the tower.

    The party climbs a reinforced broadcast tower in 3 stages. At
    each stage, the DM deploys 1 enemy from the pool. The enemies
    are ascending the tower from a different face. At each stage,
    also play the tape recorder to the party and describe something
    they have not yet done: "You hear the sound of your own voice
    screaming at a door. It is not a door you have seen yet."

    The party cannot retreat downward without forfeiting the
    scenario. Listeners have gathered at the bottom of the tower
    by the hundred. They are waiting.

    LOCAL RULE - THE CROWD IS WATCHING. After every stage, the
    crowd below grows visibly. On Stage 2, all enemies gain +1
    Attack. On Stage 3, all enemies gain +1 Attack and +1 HP. The
    broadcast is recruiting.

    SPECIAL EVENT AT STAGE 2: One party member hears a voice over
    the tape recorder that is clearly their own voice, older. It
    says one sentence: a piece of advice, a warning, or an apology.
    The DM invents it on the spot based on what the player has done
    so far. This has no mechanical effect but is mandatory.

    ACT IV - THE FINALE

    Objective: Stop the broadcast.

    At the top of the tower is a single figure at a console. They
    have the flat eyes, but there is something else underneath.
    They look up when the party arrives and smile. The smile is
    almost right. It is almost correct. It is the kind of smile
    you would get from someone who has been practicing from a
    photograph.

    When the operator speaks, they speak in a voice that every
    party member privately recognizes: the voice of someone they
    once loved. Each character hears it differently. The DM asks
    each player to name someone quietly, and then uses that name.

    The operator's stats: Attack 4, HP 6. They have one consumable
    that rolls as a 3-damage attack on any one target. They have
    a special: at the start of each round, one random party member
    must roll 1d6; on a 1 or 2, they cannot attack the operator
    that round, because they cannot bring themselves to.

    The party may:
        -   Defeat the operator in combat.
        -   Convince the operator to shut down the broadcast
            voluntarily. A character may attempt one Desperate
            check (2 or less on 1d6) per scenario. The check
            must be made in-character: the player must say
            something to the operator that the DM finds
            sufficiently true.
        -   Destroy the console instead. This is a 1-turn action
            requiring the character to stand still next to the
            console and ignore all combat. The console has HP 4.
            Any character who destroys the console dies in the
            process. Their name is remembered. Their voice is the
            last one on the tape recorder.

    AFTERMATH

    If the party ends the broadcast, the listeners below the tower
    wake up slowly. Some remember who they are. Some do not. Over
    the next days, most drift away from the tower and go home, if
    they have one. Some do not.

    The tape recorder stops playing. It is just a tape recorder
    now. The party can keep it or leave it.



SCENARIO B: THE GROCERY RUN
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    FLAVOR

    The store is still standing. That alone is miraculous. It still
    has fluorescent lights. It still has shelves. The freezer
    section still hums. No one knows why. No one wants to ask.

    The store is called BIG VALUE. Some of the letters in the sign
    are out, so depending on the angle and the time of day it reads
    BIG VAL or B  VALUE or, occasionally, just E. The parking lot
    is full of cars that have not moved in months. The cars are
    full of things that used to be drivers.

    The party is hungry. The store will have food. Other parties are
    also hungry. They will also be at the store. There will not be
    enough for everyone. The question is how much of yourself you
    are willing to leave at the checkout.

    SETUP

    Each character starts with reduced HP (current HP = maximum HP
    minus 1). This is hunger. It does not heal until the party eats
    actual food at any point during the scenario, after which all
    characters return to full HP.

    Draw 1 normal fortress from the deck as the store. Set aside 6
    entities as a pool of rival scavengers and store oddities.

    Take a small handful of item cards (around 8) and set them
    face-down as the store's stock. These will be drawn during the
    shopping action in Act II.

    Throughout this scenario, the store's PA system occasionally
    plays announcements. Use these freely as flavor: a price check
    on aisle 4 for an item that does not exist, a reminder that the
    store closes at 9 (the clock is broken and reads 9 permanently),
    a manager being called to the front, a manager being called to
    the back, a manager being called to a location with no name.
    There is no manager. There has not been a manager for a long
    time.

    ACT I - ENTRANCE

    Objective: Reach the store parking lot.

    The party moves through 3 empty streets toward BIG VALUE. At
    each street, roll 1d6 and resolve the result:

        1: A rival scavenger lurking in a doorway. Pull 1 enemy
           from the pool. Combat.
        2: A car with a passenger still strapped in, asking the
           party for directions in a polite voice. The passenger
           is not hostile. The passenger does not want directions.
           Players may ignore them, talk to them (DM improvises),
           or destroy the car (no reward, but it stops the voice).
        3: A pile of grocery bags abandoned mid-street, contents
           rotted. A character may search them for 1 random item.
           On a roll of 1, the bag is occupied.
        4: Nothing. Wind. A dog barking somewhere. The dog is
           saying words but not loudly enough to make out.
        5: A dead radio playing music that has not been written
           yet. No mechanical effect.
        6: A useful find. Draw 1 random item from the deck and
           give it to any party member.

    After the third street, the party can see the store. The
    parking lot is full. The lights are on. The doors slide open
    automatically as if welcoming them. They keep sliding open and
    closed even after the party stops moving.

    ACT II - COMPLICATION

    Objective: Get inside and load up.

    The store is held by 2 rival entities (pull from the pool) who
    have set up a checkpoint at the entrance. They are wearing red
    polo shirts they have stolen from the staff section and they
    are pretending very hard to be employees. They claim the store
    is closed for inventory. They are waiting for someone they will
    not name.

    The party may:
        -   Fight them at the entrance (standard combat).
        -   Comply with the inventory checkpoint by handing over
            1 item. The fake employees will let the party pass and
            will continue pretending until the party is out of
            sight.
        -   Hide and wait them out via a Hard check (3 or less).
            Success means slipping past unnoticed. Failure means
            combat with the fake employees, who attack first.
        -   Distract them by triggering a separate disturbance:
            knocking over a shopping cart corral, setting off the
            store alarm, etc. Allow a Normal check. On success, the
            fake employees investigate and the party slips by.

    Once inside, the store is real. Mostly. The shelves are mostly
    full. Many of the products have labels that are wrong: brand
    names spelled differently, photographs of foods the photograph
    is not actually depicting, expiration dates in years that have
    not happened. The fluorescent lights flicker only when looked
    at directly.

    SHOPPING. A character may spend one action to shop. They draw
    1 face-down item from the store stock pile. Each character may
    shop at most 3 times in this act.

    Each time a character shops, after drawing the item, roll 1d6:
        1: The aisle moves. The character finds themselves in a
           different aisle than they intended. They may shop again
           on their next action without it counting against the
           3-shop limit, but they are now separated from the party
           until they make a Normal check to find their way back.
        2: Something on a high shelf is watching. The DM describes
           it but does not attack. Yet.
        3-5: Normal shopping.
        6: Bonus. Draw a second item.

    EATING. At any point during Act II, the party may eat. This
    requires sacrificing 1 item card from the store stock (treat
    it as food regardless of what it actually is). Eating restores
    all characters to full HP. The party may only eat once per
    scenario. Eating is loud and the party is vulnerable for one
    round afterward (-1 to all attack rolls).

    AT THE BACK OF THE STORE, behind the swinging double doors
    marked EMPLOYEES ONLY, there is a freezer. The freezer is
    locked. The freezer is also vibrating slightly, in a rhythm
    that is almost a heartbeat but slower. Curious characters may
    investigate. Opening the freezer requires a Hard check or
    breaking the lock (any item used as a tool, sacrifice that
    item). Inside is one rare item card, drawn from the deck. Also
    inside is something the DM invents on the spot and the party
    does not have time to look at carefully. Whatever it is, it
    sees the party. The party should leave.

    ACT III - COLLAPSE

    Objective: Escape before rival scavengers arrive.

    Three waves of rival scavengers converge on the store. They
    have heard the party is here. Each wave is 2 enemies pulled
    from the pool. The waves enter from different sides:

        Wave 1: Through the front sliding doors.
        Wave 2: Through the loading dock at the back of the store.
        Wave 3: From above. The DM does not need to explain how.

    The party may:
        -   Defend the store. Use the store fortress as their
            position. The store fortress grants +1 Attack and +1 HP
            to defenders inside it for the duration of Act III.
            Survive all three waves to advance.
        -   Flee toward home, dropping items to move faster. Each
            item voluntarily dropped grants the dropping character
            +1 to the next round's actions. Dropped items are
            permanently lost.
        -   Split the party, with some defending and some fleeing.
            Allowed but costly: split parties roll separately for
            everything in Act IV.

    LOCAL RULE - GREED IS HEAVY. Any character carrying more than
    2 items takes a -1 penalty to all attack rolls and a -1 penalty
    to all checks while fleeing in Act III. The store's gravity
    seems to increase the more you take from it.

    SPECIAL EVENT DURING WAVE 2: The PA system makes an
    announcement, addressing the party by name, even though no one
    in this scenario knows their names. The announcement
    congratulates them on their selections and reminds them that
    the express checkout is for 10 items or fewer. Then the lights
    in the store get slightly brighter for one round. During that
    round, all characters and enemies have +1 to their Attack
    rolls. The store wants combat. The store is enjoying this.

    ACT IV - THE FINALE

    Objective: Make it home.

    The party retreats from the store with whatever they still
    carry. The walk back is shorter than the walk in, even though
    it is the same distance. The streets feel like they are
    helping.

    Each surviving character must make 1 Normal check (4 or less
    on 1d6) to reach the safehouse. Modifiers:
        -   +1 difficulty (easier) if the character is carrying 2
            or fewer items.
        -   -1 difficulty (harder) if the character is carrying 4
            or more items.
        -   -1 difficulty if the character ate during Act II.
        -   The character who opened the freezer in Act II must
            make their check at Hard (3 or less). Whatever was in
            there has decided to follow them home. They will not
            see it. They will hear it walking behind them with the
            same number of footsteps no matter how fast they run.

    Characters who fail the check are caught in an alley by a
    final rival pulled from the pool. They must fight 1 enemy
    solo. Surviving the fight completes the check.

    AFTERMATH

    Victory is measured by items successfully brought home across
    all surviving characters.

        7 or more items: A great haul. The party eats well for a
                         week. Each character permanently regains
                         1 HP up to their max if they have lost any
                         since the campaign started.
        4 to 6 items:    A good haul. The party eats. That is
                         enough.
        1 to 3 items:    A meager haul. The party eats once and
                         remembers being hungry the next day.
        0 items:         No haul. But everyone who came back came
                         back, which is more than most parties can
                         say. That counts.

    The character who opened the freezer hears something at their
    safehouse window that night. It is the sound of breathing from
    the wrong side of the glass. It is gone in the morning. Or
    it is not.



SCENARIO C: THE LAB
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    FLAVOR

    You wake up in a hallway you do not remember. There is tile
    under your face. Fluorescent light. A number printed on the
    doorframe that you are certain was different a moment ago. You
    look at it. It changes again. It is now your birthday, written
    backwards, in a font you have never seen.

    You have no weapons. Your clothes are not your clothes. They
    are the right size but they fit wrong, the way a stranger's
    shirt fits even when it is your size. Around your wrist is a
    plastic hospital bracelet with a number on it, and below the
    number, a name. The name is not yours, but you recognize it.
    You are almost sure it is the name of someone you know. You
    cannot remember who.

    A voice somewhere down the corridor is reading a list, very
    slowly, and one of the names on the list is yours. Or it is
    going to be. You cannot tell from here.

    The party does not know who they are, how they got here, or
    what happens to the people whose names get read aloud. Everyone
    agrees this is a bad sign.

    SETUP

    Each character begins with:
        -   Their base entity card (their character sheet)
        -   0 items
        -   HP at maximum
        -   A wrist bracelet (the throughline prop, see below)

    THE WRIST BRACELETS. At the start of the scenario, each player
    is told their character is wearing a hospital bracelet with a
    number and a name on it. The DM secretly assigns each character
    a bracelet name from a list. The list is the names of the OTHER
    characters in the party. Every character is wearing a bracelet
    that says someone else's name.

    The players do not learn this until the bracelets become
    relevant in Act II and Act IV. Until then, treat the bracelet
    as flavor: the character can read their bracelet, the name on
    it is a person they almost remember, and that is all.

    Draw 1 super fortress to be the Core Lab. Set aside 5 entities
    as a pool of "experiments" - things the lab has been making.
    These should not be presented as monsters at first. They should
    be presented as employees, patients, doctors, or visitors who
    are slightly wrong.

    Throughout the scenario, intercom announcements address the
    characters not by name but by their bracelet number. Use
    these freely as flavor: a request for Patient 14 to report to
    Observation, an apology that the cafeteria is closed today,
    a routine reminder that all personnel are reminded that
    contamination protocols are in effect on Sublevel 3, the
    cheerful daily affirmation read by a voice that is
    unmistakably trying its first day at being a voice.

    ACT I - ENTRANCE

    Objective: Escape your containment cell.

    Each character starts alone in a separate cell on the same
    corridor. They cannot see each other but they can hear each
    other if they shout. The cells have small windows in the doors,
    looking out onto the corridor, but the windows are clouded.

    Act I is played by each player in turn while the others wait.

    Each character must escape their cell. Two paths exist:

    PATH A - THE PHYSICAL ESCAPE. Make two Hard checks (3 or less
    on 1d6) in sequence. The first check finds something useful
    in the cell to pry with: a loose tile, a bedframe spring, a
    plastic spoon that has been sharpened by the previous
    occupant. The second check uses the tool to defeat the door
    latch.

    PATH B - THE COOPERATIVE ESCAPE. The cells are connected by
    air vents. Two characters whose players agree may attempt a
    cooperative escape. Each makes one Normal check (4 or less).
    If both succeed, they crawl out together. If one fails, both
    must make a Hard check or be discovered. The cooperative
    escape is faster but louder.

    A character who fails all attempts is discovered by a
    patrolling lab entity (pull 1 from the pool, presented as an
    orderly with a clipboard and very specific questions). They
    must defeat the orderly to escape.

    AT THE END OF ACT I: All escaped characters meet in a central
    corridor. The corridor has a wall map of the facility on it.
    The map is hand-drawn in pencil and looks like it was made
    by someone who only had access to the building one room at a
    time. Some rooms are labeled. Some are crossed out. One room
    is labeled with the name of a character who is currently
    standing in the corridor, looking at the map.

    Each character draws 1 random item from the deck. These were
    in a supply closet near the meeting point.

    ACT II - COMPLICATION

    Objective: Find your weapons, your names, and three keycards.

    The party must explore 3 lab rooms. Each room has a door with
    a different label, and each label is wrong in a different way.
    Suggested labels for the DM (use any, in any order):

        -   "JANITORIAL" (this is not a janitor's closet)
        -   "CAFETERIA - CLOSED FOR REMODEL" (it has not been a
            cafeteria for a long time)
        -   "OBSERVATION ROOM 4" (the observation goes both ways)
        -   "PHYSICAL THERAPY" (it is not therapy)
        -   "WAITING ROOM" (something is waiting)

    For each room the party enters:
        -   Draw 1 entity from the pool as the room's threat.
            Present it not as a monster but as something that
            still thinks it has a job.
        -   Draw 1 item as the room's loot.
        -   Roll 1d6 for the room's reality stability:

            1: STAT BLEED. One random character swaps one stat
               value (Attack and HP swap) until they leave the
               room. They may take this opportunity to discover
               this is sometimes useful.
            2: INVERTED PHARMACOLOGY. All item effects used in
               this room are inverted (heals damage, buffs become
               debuffs, defensive items become aggressive ones).
               The DM should narrate the inversion in-character:
               the bandage tightens too much, the painkiller
               sharpens the senses to the point of agony.
            3: ECHO ROOM. The room repeats itself once after you
               leave it. Fight the threat again, but the second
               time it knows your strategy and acts accordingly
               (DM picks better tactics).
            4: NORMAL. Nothing unusual happens. This is in some
               ways more disturbing.
            5: NORMAL. As above.
            6: MEMORY FRAGMENT. A character remembers a fragment
               of their identity. They gain +1 maximum HP
               permanently. The fragment should be specific: the
               smell of their grandmother's kitchen, the feel of
               their first car's steering wheel, the sound of a
               particular person laughing. The DM should ask the
               player to describe it.

    KEYCARDS. Each room contains 1 keycard, hidden somewhere
    appropriate to the room (in a drawer, under a body, on a
    lanyard around the neck of the threat, etc.). The party may
    or may not find each keycard. Each room with a found keycard
    grants the party 1 keycard for Act III.

    THE BRACELETS BECOME RELEVANT. Somewhere in Act II, ideally
    in the second room, the DM should describe a wall of patient
    files: photographs paperclipped to manila folders, with names
    and bracelet numbers. The players are encouraged to read their
    own bracelets aloud. They will discover their bracelets have
    other party members' names on them.

    The DM should not explain this. The DM should let the players
    sit with it for as long as it takes.

    ACT III - COLLAPSE

    Objective: Reach the Core Lab.

    The party reaches the central hub of the facility. The Core
    Lab is on the other side of a heavy security door that
    requires 3 keycards. The hallway leading to it is wider than
    the rest of the facility, ceiling tiles missing, the lights
    flickering in a pattern that is almost rhythmic, almost
    musical.

    If the party has 3 keycards, they enter directly. For each
    missing keycard, the party must make a Desperate check (2 or
    less on 1d6). Failure means fighting 1 additional entity from
    the pool as a guardian.

    LOCAL RULE - THE HALLWAY IS LEAKING. At the start of each
    round inside the hallway, roll 1d6:

        1: One random character temporarily forgets one of their
           items. The item cannot be used for that round only.
           The character also cannot describe it: they know it
           is in their pack, they know it is important, but the
           word for it has gone out of their mouth.
        2: One random character sees themselves at the end of
           the hallway, walking ahead of the party, looking
           back. The figure waves. Then it is gone. No mechanical
           effect.
        3: A door opens in the wall that was not there a moment
           ago. Inside the doorway is a room. The room is the
           bedroom of one random character, exactly as they left
           it the morning of the reality collapse. The door
           closes again at the start of the next round. A
           character may step through during the open round
           (sacrificing all actions that round) to retrieve 1
           item from their bedroom: any 1 item card from the
           graveyard, restored.
        4-6: Nothing.

    SPECIAL EVENT NEAR THE END OF THE HALLWAY: The party
    encounters a single figure standing motionless, facing the
    door to the Core Lab. The figure is wearing a lab coat and
    a wrist bracelet. The bracelet has, written on it, the name
    of a real person from the players' actual lives. The DM
    should ask each player to name a person they trust, and use
    one of those names. The figure does not respond when spoken
    to. The figure does not attack. If the party attacks first,
    the figure dies in one hit, with no resistance, and the
    party feels slightly worse afterward but continues. If the
    party ignores it and continues to the Core Lab, the figure
    is still standing there when they look back. Then it is gone.

    ACT IV - THE FINALE

    Objective: Destroy or seize the distortion machine.

    The Core Lab is the super fortress set aside at setup.

    Inside it is the machine. The machine is large and quiet. It
    does not look like science. It looks like furniture, the kind
    of furniture you find in a room that has been waiting for
    someone for a very long time. There is a chair in front of
    it. The chair has restraints. The restraints are open.

    Standing next to the machine is the boss: an entity with
    Attack 5, HP 8, and a consumable that rolls as a 2-damage
    attack on all attackers in the engagement. The boss is
    wearing a lab coat. The boss's wrist bracelet, when the party
    sees it, has the name of one of the party members on it.

    THE BRACELET REVELATION. Before the fight begins, the DM
    should tell the players what they have begun to suspect:
    each character is wearing a bracelet with the name of one
    of the others. The boss is wearing the name of one specific
    party member: the DM picks. That party member is the only
    one in the party whose bracelet does NOT have someone in
    the party on it. Their bracelet has the boss's name. They
    have always had it. They just have not read it carefully.

    The implication is left for the party to interpret. The DM
    should not explain it. Some possibilities:
        -   They are all the same person being tested.
        -   They have all been switched.
        -   The bracelets do not mean anything and the lab is
            just trying to make them think they do.
        -   They are all someone else, looking back.

    The party may:
        -   Destroy the machine. Defeat the boss in combat,
            then assault the fortress (the Core Lab) until it
            reaches 0 HP. This ends the scenario in destruction:
            the lab collapses, the party escapes, and the
            distortions in this district end. The bracelets
            crumble to dust as the party leaves.
        -   Seize the machine. Defeat the boss, but leave the
            fortress standing. The first character to touch the
            machine after the boss dies gains +1 Attack
            permanently but is now connected to it. At the
            start of every future scenario, that character must
            roll 1d6. On a 1, they glitch: lose 1 HP permanently
            and lose 1 turn at the start of the next combat.
        -   Sit in the chair. One character may volunteer to
            sit in the chair after the boss is defeated. The
            chair will not let them up. The machine will start
            doing whatever it does. The character is gone at
            the end of the scenario, but the rest of the party
            walks out clean, with no penalty, and one specific
            thing about the world is restored: the DM picks
            something small and personal to one of the
            remaining party members. A grandmother is alive
            again. A song plays on a radio that has been silent.
            A dog can no longer talk, and is happier for it.

    AFTERMATH

    Escape from the lab is automatic for all surviving characters.
    The corridors that took hours to traverse on the way in take
    minutes on the way out. The doors are unlocked. The
    fluorescent lights have begun to fail one by one as the party
    walks out. The voice on the intercom has gone silent.

    Outside, the sun is rising. None of the party can quite agree
    on whether the sun was rising when they went in. None of them
    can quite agree on how long it has been.

    The character who sat in the chair, if any, is not with the
    party when they reach the parking lot. None of the others can
    quite remember them anymore. Their items are still in the
    party's packs. Their name is still on someone's bracelet. That
    bracelet does not crumble.

    You may or may not find out who you were before this started.
    You probably won't. But you know who the others are now, and
    you know they came back for you, and that is more than most
    people in this world get to keep.

GLOSSARY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    ACT             One major segment of a Campaign Mode scenario.
                    Scenarios have 4 acts.

    ATTACK          A card's target number for normal hits. Roll this
                    value or less on 1d6 to land a hit.

    BATTLEFIELD     The central shared zone of the table where most
                    combat takes place.

    BATTLEPATH      The sequence of face-down enemy fortresses in
                    Singleplayer Mode.

    CAMPAIGN MODE   The RPG mode of Goosklerf II, run by a DM.

    CAPTURE         Taking control of an enemy fortress by clearing
                    it of defenders via a battlefield assault.

    CONSUMABLE      A one-use item card. Must be equipped by an
                    entity before it can be used.

    CONTROL         Legal ownership of a fortress, independent of
                    whether it is currently occupied.

    DEFEAT          Reducing an entity's HP to 0.

    ENGAGEMENT      A single declared combat, resolved fully before
                    any other engagement begins.

    ENTITY          A unit card. Entities fight, move, equip items,
                    and capture fortresses.

    FORTRESS        A structure card placed in the suburbs. Holds
                    entities and grants buffs. Can be captured or
                    destroyed.

    FORTRESS ASSAULT  A combat in which battlefield entities attack
                    a fortress.

    FORTRESS BARRAGE  An optional combat type in which fortress
                    entities attack an enemy fortress directly, ship
                    to ship, without a battlefield assault.

    GRAVEYARD       A player's discard pile. Each player has their
                    own.

    HAMLET          The loss-for-all end condition: no entities
                    remain and none can be played.

    HIT POINTS (HP) A card's durability, tracked physically with a
                    die on top of the card.

    INITIAL VOLLEY  A one-shot attack fortress occupants can fire
                    into a battlefield engagement at its start.

    ITEM            A card equipped to an entity, granting buffs,
                    debuffs, or special effects.

    LANDLORD        The end condition where one player controls all
                    fortresses in play with no more to come, and then
                    survives one full round without losing that status.

    LAST STAND      An optional rule allowing a defending entity to
                    take one final action upon being defeated.

    OCCUPY          Having one or more living entities physically
                    inside a fortress.

    PARRY           An optional action canceling 1 HP of normal-hit
                    damage received on the previous round.

    RARE CARD       A card with a purple, orange, or yellow/gold
                    border.

    RETREAT         An optional action to leave an engagement at the
                    cost of one round's action and exposure to an
                    enemy attack.

    SCAVENGER       An optional rule allowing allies in an
                    engagement to pick up a dead friend's items.

    SHOP            An optional face-up zone where players place 3
                    or 7 cards at setup (depending on deck regime)
                    for possible mid-game purchase.

    SUBURBS         The fortress zone at the edges of the table,
                    where fortresses are placed. The suburbs are the
                    battlefield for fortresses; the battlefield is
                    the battlefield for entities.

    SUPER ENTITY    A rare entity with 2 HP dice.

    SUPER FORTRESS  A rare fortress with 3 HP dice.

    VICTORY REWARD  A bonus random card drawn to the attacker's
                    hand after winning a battlefield engagement.
                    Defenders get nothing.



FAQ AND EDGE CASES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Q1. What if my hand has no legal plays and I must play 3 cards?

        Discard cards until played + discarded = 3. You pay the cost
        of being stuck. The forced-play rule is only waived when your
        hand is entirely empty at the start of Card Play.

    Q2. What if my deck runs out during the Draw phase?

        Draw as many as you can. If only 1 card remains, keep it. If
        0 cards remain, skip the Draw phase for the rest of the game.
        You continue playing with your current hand until it is empty.

    Q3. Does Victory Reward apply if my deck is empty?

        No. If the attacker's deck is empty, no card is drawn. You
        simply win the fight. Defenders never receive a Victory Reward.

    Q4. Can I parry a consumable's damage?

        No. Parry only cancels 1 HP of damage from a normal attack.

    Q5. A fortress has three entities inside. Do they each get an
        Initial Volley shot?

        No. Each fortress gets ONE volley shot per battlefield
        engagement, regardless of how many entities are inside. The
        controlling player picks which occupant fires.

    Q6. My fortress is currently being assaulted. Can its occupants
        volley into a different battlefield engagement happening at
        the same time?

        No. A fortress under assault cannot volley at all. Its
        occupants are already fighting the attackers at their door.

    Q7. Both sides are destroyed in the same round. What happens?

        Both sides lose. If the engagement was a fortress assault
        and the fortress still stands, it remains under defender
        control (empty). If both sides were wiped on the battlefield,
        the engagement ends with no survivors on either side. The
        Victory Reward is NOT awarded in mutual-kill scenarios.

    Q8. Can I attack my own entities or fortresses?

        Not unless a card or scenario explicitly says so.

    Q9. Can items be held in a fortress without an entity?

        No. Items must be equipped to entities. If all entities in a
        fortress die and their items are not inherited (no Scavenger,
        no Last Stand transfer), the items are discarded.

    Q10. If I purchase from the Shop and the drawn/bought card cannot
         be legally placed, what happens?

        You cannot purchase it. A Shop card must be playable on the
        spot.

    Q11. Can I heal above a card's printed HP?

        Only if a card explicitly says so. Otherwise healing caps at
        the printed value.

    Q12. I captured a fortress. When can its former occupants'
         leftover items be used?

        Never. Captured fortresses are empty of items by definition;
        all items owned by defeated defenders went to the defender's
        graveyard. You captured the real estate, not the inventory.

    Q13. My Supercharged Attack self-damaged my entity to 0. What
         happens?

        The entity is defeated by its own blunder. It is discarded
        after the action resolves. No Last Stand from a self-kill.

    Q14. A card from Goosklerf I has Defense and I am playing pure
         GKII. Do I ignore the Defense?

        You may either apply the conversion rule from Part IV, or
        ignore Defense entirely and play the card as GKII. Decide
        before the game and stay consistent.

    Q15. Can a Super Entity be parried once per HP die, or only once
         per round like a normal entity?

        Parry is an action of the parrying entity, not the entity
        being parried. A Super Entity can parry one hit per round
        like any other entity. HP dice are irrelevant to parry.

    GENERAL ART NOTES
        The setting of Goosklerf II is post-reality-collapse
        grimdark with absurd undertones. Key visual flavors for
        scenario illustrations: broken geometry, wrong animals
        in wrong places, humans who are almost human, concrete
        bureaucracy that has outlived its purpose, fluorescent
        light. The rulebook interior art should stay clean and
        functional; save the heavy mood for scenario chapter
        headers, card art, and the setting page.
